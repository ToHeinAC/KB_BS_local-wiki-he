Radon ($\text{Rn}$) is a radioactive noble gas, a chemical element with the symbol $\text{Rn}$ and atomic number 86 [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md]. It is colorless, odorless, and tasteless [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].

Radon ($\text{Rn}-222$) is a naturally occurring, colorless, odorless, and tasteless radioactive gas that emanates from the decay of radium-226 found in soil and certain building materials [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md]. Because it is invisible and undetectable by human senses, its presence and associated health risks require specialized measurement techniques [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
## Chemistry and Physics
*   **Classification:** Radon belongs to the noble gases in the 8th group of the periodic table [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Isotopes:** Naturally occurring radioactive isotopes include Radon-219, Radon-220, and Radon-222. Radon-222 is the isotope of primary concern for human radiation exposure [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Decay:** Radon-222 is formed in the decay series of Uranium-238 through several intermediate steps involving various Radium isotopes [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Hazard:** The hazard arises from its decay products (radioactive heavy metals such as Polonium, Bismuth, and Lead). When inhaled, the short-lived decay products deposit in the respiratory tract, emitting energy-rich alpha radiation ($\alpha$-Strahlung) that can damage lung cells [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].

## Occurrence and Accumulation
Radon enters buildings from multiple sources:
1.  **Subsoil:** The primary source is the emanation from the subsoil, which is influenced by the geological structure and the permeability of the subsoil [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
2.  **Building Materials:** Radon is continuously generated in mineral building materials as a decay product of Radium. While the contribution is generally small, certain materials can be significant sources [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
3.  **Other Sources:** Radon can also enter from the ambient air and groundwater, although these contributions are often considered minor compared to the subsoil source [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].

## Health Risks and Protection
*   **Health Risk:** Long-term exposure to Radon in indoor environments is demonstrably linked to an increased risk of lung cancer. The risk increases proportionally with the concentration [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Legal Threshold:** The *Strahlenschutzgesetz* sets a reference value of $300 \text{ Becquerel per cubic meter}$ for indoor air. Exceeding this value mandates protective measures [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Mitigation:** Protective measures include structural sealing against subsoil gas entry, restricting cracking in earth-contacting walls and floors, using diffusion-inhibiting materials, or implementing ventilation systems [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
## Sources and Pathways
The primary source of indoor radon is the decay of radium in the ground, leading to gas ingress into buildings [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md]. Radon can enter buildings through various pathways, including:
*   **Soil Gas Ingress:** The most common source, entering through cracks, gaps, and unsealed utility lines connecting the building to the ground [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Building Components:** Radon can diffuse through porous materials like concrete, stone, and unsealed joints in the foundation and walls [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Utility Lines:** Pipes and conduits passing from outside to inside the building must be checked to ensure they are sealed against radon gas ingress [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
## Health Risks and Assessment
Radon gas is a known cause of lung cancer, and its concentration in indoor air is a critical public health concern [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Measurement:** The presence of radon must be confirmed by measurements, typically using passive sampling methods [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Risk Assessment:** Buildings with radon concentrations exceeding 100 Bq/m³ in living areas are recommended for analysis to determine the feasibility of remediation [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
## Mitigation and Remediation
Mitigation strategies aim to reduce the concentration of radon gas by interrupting its pathways into the building envelope [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md]. The effectiveness of remediation is highly dependent on the specific measure implemented:
*   **Simple Sealing:** Can achieve reductions ranging from 0 to 25 % [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Negative Pressure in Basements:** Using small fans for basement ventilation can achieve reductions between 50 % and 80 % [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Advanced Measures:** The most effective measures involve elaborate insulation layers combined with negative pressure maintenance under the building, which can achieve reductions up to 99 % [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
## Building Physics Considerations
Effective radon protection requires treating the building as a sealed geometric body. The integrity of the building envelope is paramount. This involves:
*   **Air Tightness:** The fresh air supply must come through designated openings or ventilation systems, and the exhaust air must be clearly defined [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Structural Integrity:** All structural elements, including basement walls, floors, and utility penetrations, must be assessed for potential leakages [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
# Radon Protection Measures
Radon protection involves a multi-layered, systematic approach combining structural sealing, controlled ventilation, and engineering measures to prevent the ingress of radon gas into occupied spaces. The required measures vary significantly depending on whether the building is new construction (Neubau) or an existing structure (Bestand) [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
## Core Principles
The effectiveness of radon protection relies on synergy between different protective strategies. The planning must be systematic and competent, as the measures should be integrated into the overall building design [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
### Synergy and Conflict Potential
*   **Synergies:** Measures for moisture protection (Feuchte- und Grundwasserschutz) align completely with radon protection, as both require the sealing of leaks. Similarly, soundproofing elements can contribute to radon tightness [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Conflicts:** Caution is advised when insulating the building envelope and reducing air exchange, or when installing new heating systems without sealing potential radon pathways from the ground. In such cases, the radon concentration inside the house could exceed previously measured levels [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
## Mitigation Strategies
The remediation process is categorized into structural sealing and active ventilation systems.
### 1. Structural Sealing (Abdichtung)
Sealing is critical for both primary and secondary containment perimeters:
*   **Primary Sealing Perimeter:** Involves sealing penetrations, joints, and cracks in structural elements against earth [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Secondary Sealing Perimeter:** Involves sealing walls, ceilings, and doors between living and basement areas [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Best Practice:** The planning should aim for minimal penetrations of the building envelope through utility lines or shafts [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
### 2. Ventilation and Ventilation Systems
Ventilation is the primary active measure, requiring careful design to manage internal pressures:
*   **Pressure Management:** The goal is often to create a slight positive pressure (Überdruck) in the living areas relative to the sub-ground areas, or to manage negative pressure (Unterdruck) in the sub-ground areas [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **System Types:**
    *   **Mechanical Ventilation with Heat Recovery:** This is recommended as it allows for high air exchange rates without excessive energy loss, even in winter [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
    *   **Under-pressure Creation:** Can be achieved via dedicated ventilation systems or by creating a vacuum in the sub-ground area [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
### 3. Sub-ground Area Ventilation Techniques
Several techniques can be employed to manage soil gas:
*   **Drainage Pipes:** Collecting radon-laden soil air and venting it outside. Effectiveness depends on achieving a full-area under-pressure [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Central Collection Shafts:** Collecting soil air below the floor structure, effective if a continuous void exists or if the subsoil has high permeability (e.g., gravel) [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **External Radon Wells:** Establishing wells outside the building can lead to cost-effective remediation, as they can create a large under-pressure area in the surrounding soil [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
## Implementation Guidelines
*   **New Builds vs. Existing Buildings:** For existing buildings, it is advisable to proceed stepwise, distinguishing between "simple" and "comprehensive" remediation.
*   **Monitoring:** After construction, radon measurements are necessary to confirm the success of the measures.
