Radon ($\text{Rn}$) is a radioactive noble gas, a chemical element with the symbol $\text{Rn}$ and atomic number 86 [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md]. It is colorless, odorless, and tasteless [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

## Chemistry and Physics
*   **Classification:** Radon belongs to the noble gases in the 8th group of the periodic table [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Isotopes:** Naturally occurring radioactive isotopes include Radon-219, Radon-220, and Radon-222. Radon-222 is the isotope of primary concern for human radiation exposure [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Decay:** Radon-222 is formed in the decay series of Uranium-238 through several intermediate steps involving various Radium isotopes [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Hazard:** The hazard arises from its decay products (radioactive heavy metals such as Polonium, Bismuth, and Lead). When inhaled, the short-lived decay products deposit in the respiratory tract, emitting energy-rich alpha radiation ($\alpha$-Strahlung) that can damage lung cells [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

## Occurrence and Accumulation
Radon enters buildings from multiple sources:
1.  **Subsoil:** The primary source is the emanation from the subsoil, which is influenced by the geological structure and the permeability of the subsoil [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
2.  **Building Materials:** Radon is continuously generated in mineral building materials as a decay product of Radium. While the contribution is generally small, certain materials can be significant sources [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
3.  **Other Sources:** Radon can also enter from the ambient air and groundwater, although these contributions are often considered minor compared to the subsoil source [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

## Health Risks and Protection
*   **Health Risk:** Long-term exposure to Radon in indoor environments is demonstrably linked to an increased risk of lung cancer. The risk increases proportionally with the concentration [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Legal Threshold:** The *Strahlenschutzgesetz* sets a reference value of $300 \text{ Becquerel per cubic meter}$ for indoor air. Exceeding this value mandates protective measures [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Mitigation:** Protective measures include structural sealing against subsoil gas entry, restricting cracking in earth-contacting walls and floors, using diffusion-inhibiting materials, or implementing ventilation systems [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
