The *Radon-Handbuch_BfS* provides comprehensive information regarding the radioactive noble gas Radon ($\text{Rn}$), its natural occurrence, the associated health risks, and the necessary protective measures under German law [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

## Overview and Scope
The handbook explains how Radon, which emanates from the earth, can accumulate in buildings. A long-term stay in indoor environments with elevated Radon concentrations demonstrably increases the risk of lung cancer [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md]. Consequently, the law has incorporated Radon protection into the *Strahlenschutzgesetz* (StrlSchG), which has been fully in force since the end of 2018, alongside the new *Strahlenschutzverordnung* [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

## Radon Chemistry and Radioactivity
Radon ($\text{Rn}$) is a radioactive chemical element with the symbol $\text{Rn}$ and atomic number 86. It belongs to the noble gases in the 8th group of the periodic table [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

### Radioactivity and Decay
In nature, the radioactive isotopes Radon-219, Radon-220, and Radon-222 occur. Radon-222 ($\text{Rn}$) is particularly significant for human radiation exposure [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Decay Chain:** Radon-222 is formed in the decay series of Uranium-238 via several intermediate steps involving various Radium isotopes [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Decay Products:** The decay of Radon-222 produces several radioactive decay products, including isotopes of Polonium, Bismuth, and Lead (e.g., Polonium-218, Bismuth-214, Lead-214, Polonium-214) [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Hazard:** The decay products are radioactive heavy metals that become deposited in the respiratory tract. The energy-rich alpha radiation ($\alpha$-Strahlung) emitted during this process can damage the cells of the lung tissue [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Measurement:** The measure of activity is the Becquerel ($\text{Bq}$) [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

## Health Risks
### Lung Cancer Risk
*   **Evidence:** The link between Radon and lung cancer was first observed in the 16th century among young miners in the Erzgebirge, known as "Schneeberger Krankheit" [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Classification:** The WHO International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified Radon as definitively carcinogenic to humans in 1988 [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Risk Assessment:** Studies consistently show an increased lung cancer risk from Radon in homes [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
    *   The risk increases proportionally to the long-term Radon concentration in the apartment. Doubling the concentration doubles the risk [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
    *   There is no evidence of a threshold below which Radon would be harmless. Therefore, Radon concentration should be reduced in all apartments if feasible [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
    *   For every $100 \text{ Bq/m}^3$ of long-term Radon concentration, the lung cancer risk increases by approximately 16% [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
    *   Radon is stated to be the second most important risk factor for lung cancer after smoking [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Other Effects:** Radon can also cause radiation exposure to organs other than the lungs, particularly the nose and throat, but the resulting dose is significantly lower than that for the lungs [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

## Occurrence and Accumulation
### In Buildings
*   **Concentration:** Annual mean values for Radon activity concentration typically range between $5 \text{ Bq/m}^3$ and about $30 \text{ Bq/m}^3$. The average annual mean concentration in residential rooms in Germany is $50 \text{ Bq/m}^3$ [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Accumulation:** Higher concentrations can occur in areas where Radon concentrations in the soil air are high and good underground pathways exist, especially if the structural sealing against the subsoil is inadequate, potentially reaching several hundred $\text{Bq/m}^3$ [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Dose Calculation:** From the average Radon concentration in residential rooms of $50 \text{ Bq/m}^3$, an annual effective dose of $0.85 \text{ mSv}$ results, compared to an annual effective dose of about $0.2 \text{ mSv}$ from the average outdoor Radon concentration [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

### Sources of Radon
The sources of Radon in indoor air are listed in Table 4.1 [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md]:
| Radon Source | Typical Range | Maximum |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Subsoil | $10 – 2.000$ | $> 30.000$ |
| Building Material | $< 10 – 70$ | $< 2.000$ |
| Ambient Air | $5 – 30$ | $< 250$ |
| Water | $< 10$ | $< 100$ |

*   **Subsoil:** Radon enters primarily from the subsoil. The available amount depends on the Radon activity concentration in the soil air and the supply from the weathered area of the soil (the so-called loose zone) or along preferred pathways in the rock [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Building Materials:** Radon is continuously generated in mineral building materials as a decay product of Radium. Under normal conditions, the current building materials enclosing the room contribute at most $15 \text{ Bq/m}^3$ to the Radon concentration in the room air. However, certain materials, such as porous concrete made from alunite shale, have been shown to release significantly higher amounts [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Ambient Air:** The concentration in the open air is crucial as it represents a small but non-negligible contribution to the radiation exposure of the population [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Water:** Radon is highly soluble in water and transported by groundwater. While using water (e.g., showering) generally contributes only a few $\text{Bq/m}^3$ to the annual average, under specific geological conditions (e.g., granite areas or uranium ore deposits), individual wells could contribute up to $10 \text{ Bq/m}^3$ to the Radon concentration in residential rooms [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

## Protection Measures
### Legal Requirements
*   **General Principle:** The protection against Radon is regulated by the *Strahlenschutzgesetz* and the *Strahlenschutzverordnung* [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Reference Value:** A reference value of $300 \text{ Becquerel per cubic meter}$ is set in the law for high Radon concentrations in indoor rooms. If this value is exceeded, measures must be taken to reduce the concentration [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Mandatory Action:**
    *   **New Builds:** For new constructions in designated Radon prevention areas, builders have a fundamental duty to prevent Radon from entering the building through structural measures.
    *   **Workplaces:** If a workplace is located in a Radon prevention area, the responsible parties must measure the Radon concentration in basements and ground floors. If the concentration exceeds $300 \text{ Bq/m}^3$, immediate reduction measures must be implemented [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Residential Buildings:** In existing residential buildings, Radon protection is voluntary; the law does not impose an obligation to act [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].

### Technical Measures
*   **Ventilation:** Ventilation with widely open windows allows most Radon to escape into the outside air. The Radon concentration in an indoor room depends on the air exchange rate ($K$) and the exhalation surface area ($A$) [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
*   **Sealing:** Measures include the targeted influence of the air pressure difference between the interior and the subsoil, restricting cracking in walls and floors in contact with the earth, using diffusion-inhibiting concrete types, or extracting Radon at joints or under seals [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md [Teil 1/5].md].
