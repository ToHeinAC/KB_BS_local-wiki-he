Emergency preparedness concerning ionizing radiation is governed by a hierarchical and multi-layered system detailed within the *Strahlenschutzgesetz* (StrlSchG). The goal is to ensure coordinated, timely, and appropriate responses to potential radiological emergencies.

## Planning Structure
The law mandates the development of several interconnected plans:

1.  **General Emergency Plan of the Federation (§ 98):** This plan, adopted by the Federal Government, establishes reference scenarios and general plans for possible emergencies, including those from international organizations.
2.  **Special Emergency Plans of the Federation (§ 99):** These plans supplement the general plan for specific critical areas, such as drinking water supply, transport of goods, and contaminated areas.
3.  **State and Local Plans (§ 100):** The states are responsible for drawing up general and special emergency plans that supplement and concretize the federal plans where state authority is required.
4.  **External Emergency Plans (§ 101):** These are specific plans for the surroundings of fixed facilities with high danger potential (e.g., nuclear facilities).

## Core Principles and Procedures
*   **Emergency Protection Principles (§ 92):** These principles guide the assessment of dangers arising from emergencies involving ionizing radiation.
*   **Reference Values (§ 93, § 94):** The law defines critical thresholds, such as the reference value of 100 millisieverts for effective dose for the population, and sets dose/contamination limits for various environmental and material types (e.g., drinking water, food).
*   **Incident Management (§ 90):** Requires the radiation protection officer to report incidents, and mandates the immediate recording of personal data (name, date of birth, exposure data) for affected individuals.
*   **Training and Review (§ 102, § 103):** Authorities must conduct regular emergency exercises and regularly review and amend the plans based on real-world experiences and scientific advancements.

## Public Communication
A key component is public awareness, requiring competent authorities to:
*   Inform the public about the basic concepts of radioactivity and the potential consequences of emergencies (§ 105).
*   Ensure that information and behavior recommendations are constantly accessible and updated.
