Environmental radioactivity refers to the presence and measurement of radioactive substances in the natural environment, governed by specific federal and state monitoring mandates under the *Strahlenschutzgesetz* (StrlSchG) [StrlSchG.md].

## Monitoring Scope
The monitoring of environmental radioactivity is divided between federal and state responsibilities:

### Federal Monitoring (§ 161)
The federal government is responsible for large-scale determination of radioactivity in several matrices:
*   Air, precipitation, federal waterways, and the North and Baltic Seas outside federal waterways and in marine organisms [StrlSchG.md].
*   The ground surface, and gamma site dose rate [StrlSchG.md].
The federal government must also develop procedures for measuring and analyzing this radioactivity, compile data from various sources, and create dispersion forecasts [StrlSchG.md].

### State Monitoring (§ 162)
The states are responsible for determining radioactivity in matrices that serve as indicators for environmental radioactivity, including:
*   Food, feed, and consumer goods [StrlSchG.md].
*   Medicines and their active ingredients [StrlSchG.md].
*   Drinking water, groundwater, and surface waters outside federal waterways [StrlSchG.md].
*   Sewage, sludge, and waste [StrlSchG.md].
*   Soil and plants [StrlSchG.md].

## Data Management and Assessment
*   **Central System (§ 163):** The Federal Radiation Protection Agency operates an integrated measurement and information system to compile data from both federal and state monitoring efforts [StrlSchG.md].
*   **Evaluation (§ 164):** The Federal Ministry for Environment evaluates the data on environmental radioactivity and is required to submit an annual report on the development of radioactivity in the environment to the German Bundestag and the Bundesrat [StrlSchG.md].
## Radon-Specific Environmental Monitoring
Radon monitoring is a key component of environmental radioactivity assessment:
*   **Soil Gas:** Radon emanates from the earth, and its concentration in the soil air is measured, with typical concentrations ranging from several thousand $\text{Bq/m}^3$ up to over $1 \text{ MBq/m}^3$ in certain areas [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Water:** Radon is highly soluble in water and transported by groundwater. While the contribution from water use is generally considered minor, specific geological conditions (e.g., granite areas) can lead to contributions up to $10 \text{ Bq/m}^3$ to the indoor Radon concentration [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
*   **Air:** The concentration of Radon in the open air is important as it is a minor but non-negligible contribution to the population's radiation exposure [Radon-Handbuch_BfS.md].
