The Strahlenschutzgesetz (StrlSchG), or the Law for the Protection against the Harmful Effects of Ionizing Radiation, is the primary German legislation governing the protection of humans and the environment from ionizing radiation [summary-strlschg-01-md.md].

## Scope and Application
The law regulates protection in three main contexts:
1. Planned exposure situations.
2. Emergency exposure situations.
3. Existing exposure situations [summary-strlschg-01-md.md].

It specifies that the law applies to the exclusive economic zone and the continental shelf according to the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea [summary-strlschg-01-md.md].

## Key Definitions
The legislation establishes detailed definitions for core concepts, including:
*   **Exposure (Exposition):** The incidence of ionizing radiation on the human body, which can be external (outside the body) or internal (inside the body) [summary-strlschg-01-md.md].
*   **Radioactive Substances:** These are defined as substances containing one or more radionuclides whose activity cannot be disregarded according to the law [summary-strlschg-01-md.md].
*   **Activities (Tätigkeiten):** The law enumerates specific activities that fall under its purview, such as the handling, acquisition, storage, or processing of radioactive substances, and the operation of radiation-generating facilities [summary-strlschg-01-md.md].

## Exposure Scenarios and Categories
The law distinguishes between several types of exposure situations and categories:
*   **Expositionssituationen:** These include planned, emergency, and existing situations [summary-strlschg-01-md.md].
*   **Expositionskategorien:** These are categorized as population exposure, professional exposure, and medical exposure [summary-strlschg-01-md.md].

## Dose Measurement
The law defines methods for assessing radiation impact, including the **Effective Dose** (a weighted average of organ doses) and the **Organ-Equivalent Dose** [summary-strlschg-01-md.md].
