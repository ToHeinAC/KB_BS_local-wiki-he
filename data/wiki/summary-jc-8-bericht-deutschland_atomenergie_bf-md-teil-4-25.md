This document summarizes the findings presented in the Joint Convention on the Safety of Management of Spent Fuel and Radioactive Waste, detailing Germany's policy and procedures regarding the handling of spent fuel and radioactive waste [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].

## Scope of the Joint Convention
The Joint Convention applies to:
*   The safety of managing spent fuel originating from the operation of civil nuclear reactors [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   The safety of managing radioactive waste originating from civil applications [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   It does not apply to waste containing only naturally occurring radioactive substances (NORM) unless they are spent sources or declared as radioactive waste by the contracting party [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   It does not apply to waste from military or defense programs, unless the material is permanently transferred to exclusively civilian programs [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].

## Spent Fuel Management
### Policy and Procedures
The management of spent fuel has seen policy shifts in Germany. Previously, the utilization of fissile materials contained in spent fuel was legally mandatory until 1994. The legislation changed in 1994, allowing power plant operators the choice between reprocessing or direct final disposal [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **Reprocessing Prohibition:** Since July 1, 2005, the delivery of spent fuel from commercial electricity generation for reprocessing is prohibited according to § 9a Abs. 1 Atomgesetz (AtG) [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md]. Only direct final disposal of spent fuel located in Germany is permissible [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **Disposal:** Spent fuel should be disposed of together with high-level waste from reprocessing. The selection of a final disposal site is covered under the Site Selection Act (StandAG) [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **Export Control:** Following amendments to the AtG, the export of spent fuel from research reactors is only permissible for serious non-proliferation reasons or for sufficient supply of German research reactors for medical and other peak research purposes. An exception is the transfer for the purpose of producing waste packages suitable for final disposal in Germany [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].

### Current Storage Status (As of Report Date)
*   **Decentralized Interim Storage:** Twelve decentralized interim storage sites for spent fuel were approved, built, and operational. These are designed as dry storage facilities [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **Gorleben (BZG):** Licensed for spent fuel from LWRs and HAW glass vitrified high-level waste. The license under § 6 AtG expires in 2034, with a new application planned for 2026 [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **Ahaus (BZA):** Licensed for spent fuel from various German LWRs, plus storage for transport/storage casks for research/demonstration reactors (THTR 300, etc.) [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **Brunsbüttel (KKB):** The license was revoked by the Federal Administrative Court on January 8, 2015. Current legal basis is an administrative order under § 19 AtG until the new license application process is complete [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].

## Radioactive Waste Management
### Principles and Responsibility
*   **Polluter Pays Principle:** All steps for radioactive waste management are subject to the Polluter Pays Principle [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **State Responsibility:** The state has legally obligated waste producers to ensure the proper and safe management of radioactive waste generated during the operation and decommissioning of nuclear facilities (e.g., NPPs and research centers) [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **Funding:** The federal government assumed the financing obligation for interim and final storage of radioactive waste from the operators in 2017, utilizing a public law fund [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].

### Waste Classification
The Site Selection Act (StandAG) mandates finding a final disposal site guaranteeing optimal safety for one million years. The law distinguishes between:
*   **High-Level Waste (HAA):** Characterized by high activity concentrations and high decay heat output. Includes spent fuel, spent fuel concentrate, and decontamination/washing water [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **Low- and Intermediate-Level Waste (SMA):** Waste from operation and decommissioning, such as decommissioned plant parts, ion exchange resins, contaminated tools, and contaminated concrete structures [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].

## Technical Aspects
*   **Waste Conditioning:** Radioactive waste requires conditioning for deep geological disposal. This involves processes to ensure the waste is either directly disposable or can be treated into a disposable form [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **Storage Locations:** Interim storage is available at central and decentralized sites, including the Landessammelstellen operated by the states, and specialized interim storage facilities at research centers (e.g., Jülich, Rossendorf, Braunschweig, Geesthacht, Helmholtz-Zentrum Berlin) [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
*   **Konrad Site:** The Konrad repository is approved exclusively for radioactive waste with negligible heat development, covering weak and most intermediate-level waste [jc_8_bericht_deutschland_atomenergie_bf.md [Teil 4/25].md].
