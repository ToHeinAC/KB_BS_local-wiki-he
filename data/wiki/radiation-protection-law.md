The *Strahlenschutzgesetz* (StrlSchG) is the German federal law governing the protection against the harmful effects of ionizing radiation [StrlSchG.md [Teil 13/13].md]. It establishes comprehensive legal frameworks for managing risks associated with radiation exposure, covering everything from emergency response planning to the material composition of building resources.

## Core Principles and Scope
The law mandates detailed planning for various scenarios, including:
*   **Emergency Response:** It defines the legal foundations, tasks, and jurisdictions for the Federal and State governments, responsible authorities, and cooperating private and public organizations during emergency response [StrlSchG.md [Teil 13/13].md].
*   **Information Management:** It dictates procedures for information exchange, cooperation, assistance, and coordination during emergencies, both nationally and internationally [StrlSchG.md [Teil 13/13].md].

## Key Areas of Regulation
The StrlSchG addresses several critical areas:

### Emergency Planning and Response
The law provides detailed guidelines for emergency planning, including:
*   **Special Emergency Plans:** These plans outline specific measures for the protection of the population and emergency services, including criteria for adaptation and application of protective measures [StrlSchG.md [Teil 13/13].md].
*   **Public Communication:** It mandates structured communication protocols, ensuring the affected population receives timely information regarding the nature of the emergency, recommended behavior (e.g., hygiene, sheltering, iodine use), and instructions from authorities [StrlSchG.md [Teil 13/13].md].
*   **Warning Phases:** The law specifies recommendations to be issued even before a full emergency, such as activating communication channels and preparing public institutions [StrlSchG.md [Teil 13/13].md].

### Environmental and Material Safety
The legislation extends protection measures to the environment and construction materials:
*   **Radon Exposure:** It identifies specific workplaces, such as underground mines, radon spas, and water facilities, where increased exposure due to Radon is a concern [StrlSchG.md [Teil 13/13].md].
*   **Building Materials:** It lists radiologically relevant mineral primary raw materials (e.g., acid magmatic rocks, certain sedimentary rocks, and travertine) that must be considered when constructing buildings with living spaces [StrlSchG.md [Teil 13/13].md].
