# Strahlenschutzgesetz - StrlSchG (Teil 1/13)

This document provides a detailed summary of the initial sections of the German Radiation Protection Act (Strahlenschutzgesetz - StrlSchG), as presented in the source material [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md]. The law establishes regulations for protecting human health and the environment from the harmful effects of ionizing radiation.

## General Scope and Definitions

### Scope of Application (§ 1)
The law regulates protection against the harmful effects of ionizing radiation, specifically concerning:
1. Planned exposure situations.
2. Emergency exposure situations.
3. Existing exposure situations [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].

The law explicitly does not regulate:
* Exposure of individuals by cosmic radiation, except for flight and space personnel [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* Above-ground exposure by radionuclides naturally present in the undisturbed Earth's crust [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* Exposure by radionuclides naturally present in the human body, or by cosmic radiation near the ground [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].

### Exposure and Situations (§ 2)
The Act defines several key terms related to radiation exposure:
* **Exposure (Exposition):** The impact of ionizing radiation on the human body, whether external (outside the body) or internal (inside the body), or the measure of this impact [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* **Planned Exposure Situation (Geplante Expositionssituation):** An exposure situation arising from activities where an exposure is caused or can be caused [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* **Emergency Exposure Situation (Notfall-Expositionssituation):** An exposure situation arising from an emergency, provided the situation does not fall under Paragraph 4 [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* **Existing Exposure Situation (Bestehende Expositionssituation):** An exposure situation that already exists and requires a decision regarding its control [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* **Exposition Categories:** Three categories are distinguished: 1. Population exposure, 2. Occupational exposure, and 3. Medical exposure [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].

### Radioactive Substances (§ 3)
Radioactive substances (including nuclear fuels and other radioactive substances) are defined as all substances containing one or more radionuclides whose activity or specific activity cannot be disregarded according to the regulations of this Act or a related ordinance [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].

**Nuclear Fuels:** These include specific fissile materials such as Plutonium 239 and Plutonium 241, uranium enriched with isotopes 235 or 233, any substance containing these, or substances capable of sustaining a self-supporting chain reaction [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].

**Exclusion Criteria:** Activity or specific activity can be disregarded if the substance:
1. Falls below established release limits [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
2. Falls below established release values for a substance arising from a licensed activity, and the substance has been released [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
3. Is a substance of natural origin, not used as nuclear fuel or for generating nuclear fuel, and is not subject to monitoring under the Atomic Energy Act [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].

### Activities and Activities Types (§ 4)
**Activities (Tätigkeiten):** These encompass a wide range of actions, including:
* Handling of substances (§ 5 Abs. 39) [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* Acquisition, disposal, transport, and cross-border transfer of radioactive substances [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* Operation, testing, maintenance, or repair of X-ray or scatter beam devices [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* Adding radioactive substances to consumer goods, pharmaceuticals, or activating these products [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* Actions related to natural radioactivity that could increase exposure or contamination [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].

**Activity Type (Tätigkeitsart):** This is defined as the totality of activities that must be assessed as essentially equivalent from the perspective of the justification principle [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].

## Detailed Structure Overview

The Act is structured into several parts, covering different scenarios:

* **Part 1 (General Provisions):** Covers the basic definitions and scope [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* **Part 2 (Radiation Protection in Planned Exposure Situations):** Addresses radiation protection when exposures are planned, covering principles like justification, dose limitation, and specific technical areas (e.g., facilities for generating radiation, medical research) [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* **Part 3 (Radiation Protection in Emergency Exposure Situations):** Details protocols for managing radiation protection during emergencies, including dose and contamination values, and emergency planning [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* **Part 4 (Radiation Protection in Existing Exposure Situations):** Addresses scenarios where exposure already exists, covering topics like radioactive legacy sites, radon protection, and contaminated areas [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* **Part 5 (Cross-Cutting Provisions for Exposure Situations):** Deals with monitoring environmental radioactivity and general regulations [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* **Part 6 (Regulatory Supervision):** Covers supervisory and administrative procedures [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
* **Part 7 (Administrative Authorities):** Defines the responsibilities of various federal and state authorities [StrlSchG.md [Teil 1/13].md].
