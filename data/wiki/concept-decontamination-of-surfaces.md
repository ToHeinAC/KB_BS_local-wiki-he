# Decontamination of Surfaces

Decontamination of surfaces in radionuclide laboratories is a highly regulated process governed by standards such as DIN 25425-5. The goal is to remove radioactive contamination from rooms, work surfaces, and objects to allow for their safe reuse, while preventing the spread of contamination and the incorporation of radioactive substances [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

## Principles of Contamination Behavior
The behavior of contaminants with radioactive substances is fundamentally similar to that of non-radioactive contaminants. Contaminants can be bound to surfaces or within materials through sorption, diffusion, ion exchange, or chemical reaction [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

The difficulty of removal increases when contamination has penetrated into gaps, pores, or cracks, or when it is bound or covered by grease, dirt, or corrosion layers [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

The standard classifies contamination removal based on the mechanism of fixation:
*   **Mechanical Adhesion:** Includes simple adhesion on clean surfaces and adhesion in loose layers (e.g., dust, oil, grease, salts) [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Adsorption:** Includes physical adsorption and chemical adsorption [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Ion Exchange:** Applicable to solutions [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Diffusion:** Applicable to gases, dissolved substances, and liquids [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

## Decontamination Procedures
The procedures are categorized based on whether material removal is necessary:

### Procedures without Material Removal
These methods are suitable for contamination that is mechanically adhered, embedded in dirt layers, or physically/chemically adsorbed [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Dry Mechanical:** Vacuuming with exhaust filtration (minimum H13 efficiency per DIN EN 1822-1:2019-10) is used for dust/particles. Adhesive films can be used for weak contamination [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Wet Mechanical:** Water with additives like tensides is used. Mechanical support can involve soft plastic brushes or floor polishing machines with vacuuming [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Wet Chemical:** Use aqueous solutions of surfactant mixtures, alcohols, complexing agents, or organic acids. These must be supplemented with mechanical support [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

### Procedures with Material Removal
These are necessary if contamination is deeply embedded in pores, cracks, or gaps, embedded in corrosion layers, or diffused in surfaces [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Mechanical:** Includes cutting, milling, planing, or grinding. These must be equipped with a vacuum system. The removal depth is limited to 1 mm [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Chemical:** Use solutions of alkalis, acids, redox chemicals, or complexing agents. The surface must be carefully rinsed, neutralized, and possibly passivated after treatment [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Electrochemical:** Applicable only to metal objects. Electro-polishing uses dilute acids or mixtures thereof (usually phosphoric and sulfuric acid) to achieve a polished surface. Electro-decontaminating uses dilute acids or salt solutions, which is generally preferred due to less material removal [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

## Selection Criteria and Measurement
The selection of procedures must consider:
*   Exposure of personnel;
*   Risks from hazardous substances (according to GefStoffV);
*   Disposability of resulting waste and waste volumes;
*   Decontamination effect;
*   Minimal damage to the surface;
*   Prevention of the spread of radioactive contamination;
*   Time required for the procedure [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

Measurement of surface contamination can be done by direct and indirect methods. Indirect methods (like wipe tests) are associated with uncertainties and can only allow for a conservative estimate. For compliance outside radiation protection areas, adherent contamination can be disregarded [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
