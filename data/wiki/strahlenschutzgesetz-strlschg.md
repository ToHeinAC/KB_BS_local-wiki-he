The Strahlenschutzgesetz (StrlSchG), or Law for the Protection against the Harmful Effects of Ionizing Radiation, is the comprehensive German legislation governing the protection of humans and the environment from ionizing radiation. The law, effective as of June 27, 2017, establishes detailed rules for the handling, use, and disposal of radioactive substances and the control of radiation exposure.

### Scope and Purpose
The primary purpose of the StrlSchG is to protect against the harmful effects of ionizing radiation [StrlSchG.md [Teil 11/13].md]. The law details administrative requirements, including the necessity for permits, notifications, and adherence to strict safety standards across various sectors, such as medical research, industrial applications, and waste management [StrlSchG.md [Teil 11/13].md].

### Key Regulatory Areas
The Act establishes specific competencies among several federal authorities:
*   **Federal Radiation Protection Agency ($\S 185$):** This agency is responsible for critical areas, including granting permits for the application of radioactive substances or ionizing radiation to humans for medical research, managing registers of ethics committees and professional exposures, and overseeing high-radioactive sources [StrlSchG.md [Teil 11/13].md].
*   **Federal Office for the Safety of Nuclear Waste ($\S 186$):** This office manages the permitting and transport control for large sources of radioactive material [StrlSchG.md [Teil 11/13].md].
*   **Federal Physical-Technical Institute ($\S 187$):** This institute is tasked with type approval for scatter sources and providing radioactivity standards for comparison measurements [StrlSchG.md [Teil 11/13].md].
*   **Jurisdictional Distribution:** Competencies are distributed among various bodies, including the State Authorities ($\S 184$), the Federal Aviation Office ($\S 189$), and the Federal Office for Economic Affairs and Export Control ($\S 188$) [StrlSchG.md [Teil 11/13].md].

### Administrative Requirements
The law mandates strict administrative compliance:
*   **Documentation:** Permits and approvals must generally be issued in writing, and electronic versions require a qualified electronic signature [StrlSchG.md [Teil 11/13].md].
*   **Transitional Rules:** The Act contains detailed transitional provisions ($\S 196$ to $\S 201$) ensuring that permits and operations authorized before December 31, 2018, remain valid under the new legal framework [StrlSchG.md [Teil 11/13].md].
*   **Enforcement:** Violations of the Act constitute administrative offenses ($\S 194$), carrying potential fines up to one hundred thousand Euros, depending on the nature of the violation [StrlSchG.md [Teil 11/13].md].
