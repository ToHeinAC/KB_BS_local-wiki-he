# DIN 25425-5:2024-12: Radionuclide Laboratories – Part 5: Rules for the Decontamination of Surfaces

This document, DIN 25425-5:2024-12, provides general rules for carrying out decontamination measures in radionuclide laboratories for both adherent and non-adherent surface contaminations, aiming to allow the reuse of rooms, work surfaces, and objects [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

## Overview and Scope
The standard is applicable to contaminations in rooms and on work surfaces within radionuclide laboratories, and also to surfaces of objects from these areas [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

The DIN 25425 series is composed of the following parts:
*   Part 1: Rules for the Design (Auslegung);
*   Appendix 1 to Part 1: Examples of Implementation (Ausführungsbeispiele);
*   Part 3: Rules for Preventive Fire Protection (Vorbeugender Brandschutz);
*   Part 4: Rules for Personal Protection (Personenschutz);
*   Appendix 2 to Part 4: Assistance for Dimensioning Shielding of Photon and Beta Radiation;
*   Part 5: Rules for the Decontamination of Surfaces (Rules for the decontamination of surfaces) [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

## Contamination Behavior (Section 4)
The behavior of contaminants with radioactive substances is fundamentally not different from that of contaminants with non-radioactive substances. Contaminants can be bound to surfaces or in material by sorption, diffusion, ion exchange, or chemical reaction [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

Decontamination is complicated if the contamination has penetrated into gaps, pores, or cracks, or if it is bound or covered by grease, dirt, or corrosion layers [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

**Table 1 — Decontamination depending on the type of fixation of contamination** details the mechanisms:
*   **1 Mechanical Adhesion:** Includes simple adhesion on clean surfaces (dry/wet mechanical) and adhesion in loose layers (e.g., dust, oil, grease, salts) [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **2 Adsorption:** Includes physical adsorption (simple, wet chem. with mechanical support) and chemical adsorption (mostly difficult, mostly wet chem. with mechanical support) [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **3 Ion Exchange:** Applicable to solutions (mostly wet chem. with carrier solution for isotope exchange) [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **4 Diffusion:** Applicable to gases, dissolved substances, and liquids (mostly with material removal: mechanical, chemical, electrochemical; for Tritium: outgassing) [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

## Decontamination Procedures (Section 7)

### 7.2 Procedures without Material Removal
These procedures are suitable for contamination that is mechanically adhered to clean surfaces, embedded in dirt layers, or physically/chemically adsorbed [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

*   **Dry Mechanical Procedures:** Suitable for dust or particles. Dust must be vacuumed with exhaust filtration, requiring filter element efficiency of at least H13 according to DIN EN 1822-1:2019-10 [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md]. Adhesive films can be used for weak contamination and low dust amounts [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Wet Mechanical Procedures:** Water with additives like tensides is generally used. Mechanical support can involve soft plastic brushes or floor polishing machines with vacuuming [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md]. Ultrasound can improve cleaning, especially on rough surfaces, using a shaking bath or encapsulated sonotrode [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Wet Chemical Procedures:** Use aqueous solutions of surfactant mixtures, alcohols, complexing agents, or organic acids. These must be supplemented with mechanical support such as wiping, brushing, rinsing, or ultrasound [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

### 7.3 Procedures with Material Removal
These are required if contamination is deeply embedded in pores, cracks, or gaps, embedded in corrosion layers, or diffused in surfaces [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

*   **Mechanical Procedures:** Includes cutting, milling, planing, or grinding. These must be equipped with a vacuum system. The removal depth must be uniform and may reach several millimeters. This method is only suitable for removal depths up to 1 mm [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Chemical Procedures:** Use solutions of alkalis, acids, redox chemicals, or complexing agents. The application of different solutions in alternation can be beneficial. After decontamination, the surface must be carefully rinsed, neutralized, and possibly passivated [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
*   **Electrochemical Procedures:** Applicable only to metal objects. The object is connected as the anode in the electrolyte bath. The metal and contamination are removed by applying a direct current. Electro-polishing uses dilute acids or mixtures thereof (usually phosphoric and sulfuric acid) to achieve a polished surface [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md]. Electro-decontaminating uses dilute acids or salt solutions, resulting in less material removal and generally being preferred in practice [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

## Selection Criteria and Measurement (Sections 8 & 9)
The selection of procedures must consider:
*   Exposure of personnel;
*   Risks from hazardous substances (according to GefStoffV);
*   Disposability of resulting waste and waste volumes;
*   Decontamination effect;
*   Minimal damage to the surface;
*   Prevention of the spread of radioactive contamination;
*   Time required for the procedure [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].

Measurement of surface contamination can be done by direct and indirect methods. Indirect methods (like wipe tests) are associated with uncertainties and can only allow for a conservative estimate. For compliance outside radiation protection areas, adherent contamination can be disregarded [DIN 25425-5_2024-12.md].
